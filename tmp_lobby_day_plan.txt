National Advocacy Day on Capitol Hill: Complete Plan and Execution Package for a New fs2shub.com Event Tab
Executive summary
This package provides a complete event plan and implementation blueprint to launch a National Advocacy Day on Wednesday, October 21, 2026 (8:30 a.m.–3:30 p.m.) on Capitol Hill, centered on supporting incarcerated and returning opportunity/justice-involved youth and young adults through education and cross-system supports. The proposed design aligns with Convening themes described publicly for “From Silos to Solutions,” which emphasizes collaboration across educators, policymakers, and stakeholders to advance bold policy reform and inclusive systems for justice-involved youth and young adults. [1]
The recommended event structure is a Capitol Hill briefing + advocacy training + scheduled congressional office meetings + structured debrief. This format is designed to be realistic within Capitol Hill operational constraints, especially those governing use of United States Capitol Visitor Center[2] spaces (member sponsorship, legislative nexus, occupancy limits, restrictions on fundraising/charging, promotional limits, and possible short-notice changes based on congressional business). [3]
Key operational constraints that shape the plan:
	•	CVC rooms/spaces: must be requested by a current Member of Congress or Officer of Congress, must have a clear legislative connection, requests can be made up to 4 months in advance, and CVC may cancel/move events on short notice due to congressional business. [4]
	•	No fees/fundraising in CVC: no charge may be levied on participants; no money or items may be collected; no fundraising permitted. [5]
	•	CVC A/V, press, vendors: press conferences are not permitted; press must be coordinated through House/Senate Radio/TV galleries; vendors need approval lead time and insurance requirements may apply. [6]
	•	Capitol Grounds demonstrations: events may require permitting; accessibility routes must remain unblocked; temporary structures and shelters are prohibited; sign supports and sound amplification have restrictions. [7]
	•	Visitor screening & prohibited items: all visitors are screened by magnetometer/x-ray; plan for delays and strict prohibited-items rules (including limitations on bags, food/beverage, and sign supports). [8]
Recommended capacity planning:
	•	Core Advocacy Briefing in CVC auditorium: up to 450 (theater style) with no food/beverage in the auditorium. [9]
	•	Smaller training breakouts in CVC meeting rooms (82 each) or off-site partner space if breakout capacity is constrained. [9]
	•	Hill meetings: capacity is governed by meeting scheduling bandwidth; this plan assumes a primary participant cap of ~150–200 to ensure quality meetings, staffing coverage, and accessible support (assumption; refine once staffing level and meeting count targets are set).
Assumptions (explicit because not specified):
	•	Exact reserved spaces (CVC vs House office buildings vs off-site) are not yet confirmed; plan includes decision gates and fallbacks. [10]
	•	Budget cap is not provided; a scalable budget with ranges is included.
	•	Confirmed “Convening” agenda integration and attendee pipeline are not provided; messaging and schedules assume this Advocacy Day is an official Convening track and should be marketed accordingly with proper approvals. [11]
	•	Direct access to fs2shub.com content was blocked in research (site returned access errors), so the web implementation portion is designed as a stack-agnostic blueprint with two implementation paths (CMS-first and code-first). (Observed limitation; no public citation.)
Success metrics (KPIs) are built around: participant conversion, diversity and lived-experience participation, number/quality of Hill meetings, advocacy actions completed, post-event commitments/follow-ups, and satisfaction/accessibility performance, supported by privacy-appropriate analytics and structured evaluation. [12]
Event strategy and format options
Purpose and goals
Purpose: Convene and equip a cross-sector coalition—especially people with lived experience of incarceration and reentry—to advocate for policies and funding that strengthen educational access, reentry supports, and system coordination for opportunity/justice-involved youth and young adults. This purpose aligns with the publicly described “From Silos to Solutions” convening intent to coordinate supports for justice-involved youth and adults through collaboration and policy reform. [1]
Goals (measurable): 1. Policy engagement: secure scheduled meetings with congressional offices and deliver a consistent, values-based policy message with clear requests (targets set in planning). 2. Capacity building: train participants in advocacy meeting structure, trauma-informed storytelling, and “the ask,” emphasizing safe participation for impacted youth. 3. Coalition building: strengthen cross-system coordination among educators, reentry providers, youth-serving orgs, and philanthropic partners aligned to opportunity/justice-involved communities. [1] 4. Visibility (optional): conduct a compliant, permitted visibility moment (photo or rally) that does not violate Capitol Grounds rules or CVC promotional restrictions. [13] 5. Sustained follow-through: convert Advocacy Day into ongoing action (district follow-ups, list building, and post-event campaigns) with compliant email and privacy practices. [14]

Roles and staffing needs
This staffing model emphasizes (1) Hill meeting execution quality, (2) safety/accessibility, and (3) high-fidelity data capture.
Core roles:
	•	Event Director (overall accountability): owns run-of-show, decision gates, and congressional sponsor liaison.
	•	Hill Meetings Lead: manages meeting request list, confirmations, team assignments, and real-time changes.
	•	Training Lead: develops facilitation guide, role-play scripts, and briefing content.
	•	Registration & Data Lead: owns attendee data, check-in, meeting outcome capture, and analytics reporting (with privacy-by-design controls aligned to risk management frameworks). [20]
	•	Accessibility Lead: coordinates reasonable accommodations and effective communication (auxiliary aids/services when needed). [21]
	•	Safety & Compliance Lead: coordinates with venue policies, ensures compliance with Capitol Grounds restrictions if any outdoor activity is planned. [22]
	•	Volunteer Coordinator: recruits/trains volunteers and assigns positions (wayfinding, check-in, runners).
	•	Communications Lead: outreach, partner toolkits, social content, and press handling aligned to venue restrictions (e.g., CVC press-conference ban). [16]
	•	A/V & Tech Lead (day-of): microphones, slides, accessibility tech, and live troubleshooting.
Recommended staffing quantities (scalable target ranges; assumptions):
	•	Team Captains: 1 captain per delegation team (ideally 8–12 attendees/team).
	•	Meeting Schedulers (pre-event): 1–2 staff dedicated in the final 6–8 weeks.
	•	Check-in & wayfinding: 6–12 volunteers depending on venue complexity.
	•	Accessibility support: 1 dedicated point person + 1 floating assistant; coordinate with Office of Congressional Accessibility Services[23] for Capitol complex-specific supports (adaptive tours, sign language interpreting services, wheelchair loans). [24]

[30] - Press conferences in CVC are not permitted; press-related activity must be coordinated via House and Senate Radio/TV galleries. [31]
Public health & medical readiness (proportionate to scale): - Use a risk-based approach to mass-gathering health (e.g., infectious disease considerations are known risks at mass gatherings). [32] - Maintain a basic first-aid plan (onsite first-aid supplies, emergency contact protocol, trained staff/volunteer point person), consistent with widely used special-event risk guidance. [33]

Ticket types, capacity limits, and waitlist policy
Because charging participants can be prohibited in CVC (and fundraising is prohibited), the default ticket model should be $0 tickets used purely for capacity management and communication. [5]
Recommended ticket types (all $0): - General Attendee (partner org / educator / provider) - Youth & Young Adult Participant (lived experience / student / alumni) - Congressional Staff (observer) - Volunteer / Runner - Media Inquiry (intake only; attendance subject to approval and venue rules)
Capacity model (set caps by “critical constraint,” not aspirational marketing): - Cap Confirmed Registration to the smallest guaranteed briefing/training space or to Hill meeting scheduling bandwidth (whichever is smaller). CVC rooms have published capacity limits that cannot be exceeded. [42]
Waitlist policy (recommended): - Waitlist turns on automatically at cap. - Prioritization tiers (transparent, values-aligned): (1) directly impacted youth/young adults, (2) geography balance across states/districts, (3) essential partners (education + reentry), (4) general attendees. (Assumption; document publicly.) - Release waitlist seats in weekly waves starting 4 weeks out, then daily in final week. - Require confirmation within 24–48 hours; otherwise seat rolls.

Measurement and post-event evaluation plan
Metrics and KPIs
KPIs should be tracked at three levels: (1) participation, (2) policy engagement, and (3) systems-building.
Participation - Total registrations; attendance rate; waitlist conversion - Representation of directly impacted youth/young adults (self-identified and optional) - Accommodation requests fulfilled (count + satisfaction) consistent with effective communication expectations. [25]
Policy engagement - Number of Hill meetings completed - Number of offices attending briefing (if applicable) - Number of offices requesting follow-up information - Concrete outcomes: commitments, letters, co-sponsorship exploration, staff touchpoints captured (qualitative + structured)
Communications - Email list growth (update list sign-ups) and opt-out compliance (opt-out processing expectations are explicit for covered commercial email; adopt as standard practice). [61] - Social reach and engagement for campaign hashtags (if used)
Operational quality - On-time start rate; schedule adherence - Incident log count (security, accessibility, health) and resolution time - “Know Before You Go” effectiveness (self-reported: screening friction, prohibited items issues). [8]
Post-event evaluation methods
Within 24 hours - Send a short survey (5–10 questions) segmented by role (youth vs provider vs educator). - Require structured meeting-note submission from captains (3 prompts: what was asked, response, next step).
Within 10 business days - Conduct a cross-team debrief focused on: what worked, what failed, what to change—consistent with special event guidance emphasizing planning, stakeholder coordination, and after-action learning. [65] - Publish a brief internal “Advocacy Day outcomes memo” (for partners and funders) that avoids restricted promotional references if any activities were in CVC spaces. [11]
Within 30–60 days - Track follow-ups: meeting thank-yous, district meeting scheduling, campaign actions, and policy progress updates. - Maintain privacy and security controls consistent with widely used cybersecurity/privacy frameworks for managing risk around stored personal data. [66]
